# How to Set Up Mountain Bike Suspension

Source: https://www.liv-cycling.com/global/campaigns/how-to-set-up-mountain-bike-suspension/20654

## MTB Suspension 101: SAG and Rebound

Riding a full-suspension mountain bike instead of a hardtail generally allows you to traverse technical terrain with less effort and maintain better traction on rough or loose surfaces—but only with proper suspension setup. Poor suspension configuration is a primary reason many riders experience suboptimal performance on their bikes.

### Suspension Terms

**Travel**: The amount of suspension compression available, measured in millimeters. For example, the Liv Hail offers 160mm while the Pique provides 120mm.

**Fork**: Front suspension on a mountain bike.

**Shock**: Rear suspension on a mountain bike.

**Stanchion**: The polished component of the fork or shock that extends into the fork lowers or shock body.

**Sag**: The travel consumed simply by sitting on the bike. A sag test measures adequate air pressure in your fork or shock. Ideally, you should compress 25-35% of total travel when seated.

**Shock Pump**: The specialized device for pressurizing suspension components. Standard bike tire pumps are incompatible.

**Compression Damping**: A control knob on some forks and shocks regulating oil flow through internal ports. Greater compression damping produces a firmer feel.

**Rebound Damping**: This adjustment controls how quickly your fork or shock returns to its resting position. Increased rebound damping slows the return speed.

## How to Set Sag on a Mountain Bike

1. Recruit a friend to stabilize your front wheel during testing.

2. If your suspension includes compression damping knobs, position them fully open.

3. Stand on the pedals and bounce several times while applying slight front-end pressure, mimicking riding conditions. This mobilizes the internal oil and prevents stiction.

4. While maintaining your typical riding position, have your assistant reset the rubber o-rings on your stanchion.

5. Gently lower yourself onto the saddle, then dismount.

6. Examine your stanchion. The o-ring should sit approximately one-quarter down from the top. If it's positioned less than one-quarter down, you have excessive air pressure—use your shock pump to release air. If positioned more than one-quarter down, you lack sufficient pressure—add air accordingly.

## How to Adjust Rebound Damping

After confirming correct sag, proceed to rebound adjustment. Rebound tuning remains highly personal, yet improper settings significantly compromise riding quality. As a starting point, determine your total available clicks by rotating the rebound knob fully counterclockwise (toward the jackalope symbol on RockShox or minus sign on Fox) to eliminate all damping. Count clicks while rotating clockwise (toward the turtle on RockShox or plus sign on Fox). Return halfway through your count.

Test the rebound feel by compressing and releasing either the saddle (for shock rebound) or handlebars (for fork rebound) using open-palm pressure without gripping. Your suspension should return promptly to center without bouncing excessively. Adjust clicks as needed. "Slower" rebound requires additional damping (turtle/plus), while "faster" rebound needs less damping (jackalope/minus).

Fine-tuning happens best during actual riding. If your bike feels like it's launching you on landings or the ride feels harsh over technical features, reduce rebound speed. If the suspension "packs up" over consecutive bumps and fails returning to neutral, increase rebound speed.

## When to Use Compression Damping

Every fork and shock differs in damping options—some offer high and low-speed compression settings, others provide two or three options, and some have none. Review your specific suspension manufacturer's documentation and recommendations.

One key consideration: if your suspension provides a lockout compression position, use it exclusively on roads, gravel, or smooth singletrack. With correctly configured sag, maintain active suspension while climbing technical terrain. Suspension assists traction when ascending rocks and roots just as it does descending—keeping tires grounded improves traction throughout.

Suspension setup involves considerable technical depth. The primary advice: experiment with your fork and shock adjustments. Learn what each control does and discover your preferred settings through hands-on testing.
